# Easter and Passover: The Shared Story of Redemption That Unites Two Faiths

Passover begins at sundown on April 5, just days after Easter. This proximity is not coincidental — it is embedded in the very origins of the Christian faith. To understand Easter more deeply, Christians must understand Passover. The two are inseparable.

## The Lamb of God and the Passover Lamb

When John the Baptist saw Jesus approaching the Jordan River, he declared: "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" (John 1:29). This was not abstract poetry. Every Jewish person who heard it immediately understood the reference: the Passover lamb.

In Exodus, God commanded the Israelites to sacrifice an unblemished lamb and mark their doorposts with its blood so that the angel of death would "pass over" their homes. The blood of the lamb was the sign of protection. The lamb died so that the firstborn would live.

The New Testament writers are explicit and deliberate in drawing this parallel. Paul writes in 1 Corinthians 5:7: "Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed." The Gospel of John places the crucifixion specifically at the hour when Passover lambs were being slaughtered in the Temple. Peter describes Christ as "a lamb without blemish or defect" (1 Peter 1:19), directly echoing the Passover requirement.

Jesus is not just described using Passover imagery — he is presented as the fulfillment of Passover. The original Passover pointed forward to him. His sacrifice is the substance to which the lamb was the shadow.

## The Last Supper Was a Seder

The meal that Christians know as the Last Supper was a Passover Seder. Jesus and his disciples gathered on the eve of Passover to observe the ancient feast that commemorated Israel's liberation from Egypt. Everything on that table — the matzah, the wine, the bitter herbs, the retelling of the Exodus — was the Seder.

When Jesus took bread and broke it saying "This is my body, given for you," he was adapting the matzah of the Seder. When he took the cup of wine and declared "This cup is the new covenant in my blood, shed for you," he was taking one of the traditional Passover cups and giving it new meaning. The Seder was not replaced — it was fulfilled, reinterpreted, and carried forward.

This is why understanding the Passover Seder is not optional for Christians who want to understand the Lord's Supper. Every element of Communion has roots in the Passover table. The bread, the wine, the covenant — all of it comes from there.

## From Exodus to Resurrection: The Shape of Salvation

The shape of the Passover story and the shape of the Easter story are identical:
- The people are enslaved and cannot free themselves
- God intervenes with power that surpasses any earthly force
- A sacrifice is required — blood is shed
- The people are brought through death to new life on the other side
- A covenant is established between God and his people

In the Exodus, Israel passed through the Red Sea — the waters of death parted and they emerged on the other side, free and alive. Paul interprets this directly in 1 Corinthians 10: they were "baptized into Moses in the cloud and in the sea." The crossing of the sea was a foreshadowing of baptism, which Paul describes as passing through death and rising to new life with Christ (Romans 6:4).

Every generation of Israel retold the Exodus at Passover and was commanded to see themselves as if they personally left Egypt. Every generation of Christians retells the death and resurrection of Jesus in Communion and baptism and is called to see themselves as dying with Christ and rising with him.

The stories are not parallel by accident. They are the same story told twice — once in shadow, once in substance.

## What This Means for Christians Today

Understanding this connection should deepen your worship in at least three ways:

**Communion becomes more powerful** when you understand that you're participating in a meal that goes all the way back to Egypt — that every Passover Seder for 1,400 years between the Exodus and the crucifixion was pointing forward to this moment.

**The Old Testament becomes more alive** when you stop reading it as a collection of historical documents from an earlier religion and start reading it as the first half of one story whose second half is the New Testament. The Passover lamb was always a signpost.

**Your understanding of salvation deepens** when you see that God has always been in the business of freeing enslaved people by grace, through the blood of a substitutionary sacrifice, across an impossible boundary, into a covenant relationship. This is not Christianity's invention. It's God's character, revealed progressively across the whole story of Scripture.

This Passover and Easter season, read Exodus alongside the Gospel accounts. Sit with the Passover Seder and the Last Supper together. The depth you find there will enrich your faith in ways that isolated reading never will.

The story is one. The Redeemer is one. The rescue is real.
